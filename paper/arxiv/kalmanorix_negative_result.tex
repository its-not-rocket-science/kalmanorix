\begin{abstract}
This paper reports a powered negative result from a domain-balanced retrieval benchmark designed for inferential reliability rather than optimistic effect discovery. On a 1,193-query paired test set (validation split: 634; minimum per-domain test count: 126), using the latest canonical domain-balanced configuration (\texttt{domain-balanced=true}, \texttt{max\_candidates=100}), uncertainty-weighted Kalman-style fusion and mean fusion are indistinguishable on the primary metric nDCG@10 (Kalman-minus-Mean delta $-9.258801070226193\times10^{-6}$). Holm-adjusted p-values are 1.0, estimated effect sizes are negligible, and confidence intervals are consistent with approximately zero practical difference. Secondary metrics agree with this null pattern (recall@100 delta 0.0). Compute overhead is bounded and acceptable (latency ratio Kalman/Mean $\approx$1.0722551296204925; FLOPs ratio 1.0), so the result is not confounded by prohibitive runtime distortion. The benchmark verdict is \texttt{inconclusive\_sufficiently\_powered}: the study had enough power to detect practically meaningful effects under pre-registered thresholds, but no such effect appeared. We therefore frame the contribution as a reproducible evaluation methodology---paired testing, multiplicity control, domain balancing, and uncertainty-aware reporting---with Kalman fusion as an experimental vehicle rather than a superiority claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Retrieval papers often report small gains under limited test budgets, then interpret those gains as architectural progress. This pattern is vulnerable to underpowering, selective metric emphasis, and multiplicity effects. Our goal is to invert that incentive structure: prioritise inferential robustness first, then discuss models.

We present the final Kalmanorix mixed-domain benchmark outcome as a powered negative result. Under a claim-ready data regime (1,193 paired test queries, balanced domain coverage, paired analysis), Kalman-style uncertainty-weighted fusion does not improve over mean fusion on nDCG@10. Adjusted p-values (Holm) are 1.0, effect sizes are negligible, and confidence intervals include practically null effects.

This does not establish that uncertainty-aware fusion is universally ineffective. It establishes that, in this benchmarked setting, expected retrieval gains are too small to separate from zero despite sufficient power for pre-specified effect scales. We therefore reposition the paper around \emph{evaluation discipline}: uncertainty-aware benchmarking, explicit multiplicity control, and compute-aware reporting.

\section{Related Work}
The methodological ingredients are standard. Kalman filtering originates in linear estimation theory \cite{kalman1960}. Retrieval quality is assessed with graded-relevance ranking metrics such as nDCG \cite{jarvelin2002}. BEIR motivates heterogeneous zero-shot retrieval benchmarking \cite{thakur2021beir}. For inference under paired query outcomes we use Wilcoxon signed-rank testing \cite{wilcoxon1945}, bootstrap confidence intervals \cite{efron1994}, and Holm correction for family-wise error control \cite{holm1979}.

Because representation quality can dominate downstream fusion effects, we separate this fast-local deterministic embedding setting from neural-encoder regimes (e.g., Sentence-BERT \cite{reimers2019}).

\section{Benchmark and Experimental Protocol}
\subsection{Data and benchmark construction}
The benchmark manifest (\texttt{mixed\_beir\_v1.2.0}) is assembled from BEIR-style subsets including NQ, SciFact, FiQA, ArguAna, FEVER, and DBPedia Entity. The canonical split used here contains train=5078, validation=634, and test=1193 queries, with minimum per-domain test count of 126. The final claim-ready run is explicitly domain-balanced with \texttt{max\_candidates=100}.

\subsection{Primary estimand and powered design}
The primary estimand is the paired difference in nDCG@10 between KalmanorixFuser and MeanFuser on the test split. The benchmark is designed to support claim-ready inference through query-level pairing, balanced domain coverage, and minimum sample constraints.

\subsection{Statistical analysis plan}
We report:
\begin{itemize}
    \item paired tests on query-level outcomes,
    \item Holm-corrected p-values across the pre-declared family,
    \item effect-size estimates,
    \item bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals,
    \item compute-side diagnostics (latency and FLOPs ratios).
\end{itemize}

This analysis plan treats uncertainty quantification as first-class output, not appendix material.

\section{Methods and Scope}
\subsection{Systems compared}
We compare (i) \textbf{MeanFuser}, (ii) \textbf{KalmanorixFuser} (uncertainty-weighted fusion), and (iii) a \textbf{hard-routing baseline}. Kalman fusion is used here as an experimental probe of whether uncertainty weighting produces measurable gains under strict evaluation.

\subsection{Compute-aware orchestration perspective}
Beyond quality metrics, we track latency and FLOPs behavior to ensure conclusions are not detached from deployment constraints. This supports a compute-aware orchestration framing: evaluation should jointly report effectiveness and operational cost.

\subsection{Why pilot-scale improvements disappeared under powered evaluation}
Several mechanisms plausibly explain why early, smaller-sample improvements did not survive final evaluation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Underpowered pilot benchmarks.} Small query sets inflate variance and produce unstable rank-ordering among close methods.
    \item \textbf{Multiple hypothesis testing.} Without strict multiplicity correction, nominal wins are expected by chance when many metrics/slices are inspected.
    \item \textbf{Fragile small retrieval gains.} Effects near zero are highly sensitive to query mix, domain composition, and relevance-label noise.
    \item \textbf{Publication bias in IR benchmarking.} Positive pilot outcomes are more likely to be written up than null outcomes, exaggerating expected effect magnitudes in the literature.
    \item \textbf{Benchmark contamination concerns.} Repeated tuning against familiar benchmark families can leak evaluation signal into design choices, shrinking external validity of apparent gains.
\end{itemize}

These points motivate powered, domain-balanced, uncertainty-first reporting as a default, especially when claimed gains are small.

\subsection{Distinguishing diagnostic runs from the canonical result}
Earlier diagnostic experiments (including smaller c40 and non-balanced settings) were used for debugging and sensitivity checks, not final inference. Those runs can show larger apparent deltas because of reduced sample size and composition drift. The final reported claim is based only on the canonical C100, domain-balanced, \texttt{max\_candidates=100} configuration with \texttt{n\_pairs=1193}.

\section{Discussion}
The substantive contribution is not that one fusion rule ``wins.'' Instead, the contribution is a replicable retrieval evaluation workflow that makes negative outcomes informative: paired inference, Holm correction, effect-size reporting, confidence intervals, domain-balance constraints, and compute-aware diagnostics.

From this perspective, Kalman fusion is a useful \emph{instrument} for stress-testing methodology. A method that is intuitively appealing but empirically null under powered evaluation helps calibrate the field against over-interpretation of pilot results. This is valuable for reproducibility-focused venues (e.g., TMLR/SIGIR reproducibility tracks), where disciplined uncertainty accounting is central.

\section{Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Configuration scope.} Results are bounded to this mixed-domain benchmark and fast-local deterministic embedding regime.
    \item \textbf{Mechanistic attribution.} We identify statistical non-separation, not the full causal mechanism of fusion failure.
    \item \textbf{Benchmark-family dependence.} Even domain-balanced designs can inherit biases from dataset family construction and relevance judgments.
    \item \textbf{Generalisation beyond tested compute envelopes.} ``Acceptable'' latency/FLOPs ratios are environment-dependent and may shift under different hardware or indexing stacks.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
On a 1,193-query powered, domain-balanced benchmark (canonical C100 with \texttt{max\_candidates=100}), uncertainty-weighted Kalman fusion does not show a statistically or practically meaningful nDCG@10 improvement over mean fusion (delta $-9.258801070226193\times10^{-6}$). Adjusted p-values are 1.0, effect sizes are negligible, and confidence intervals support a near-zero delta interpretation. The correct reading is a powered negative result: \texttt{inconclusive\_sufficiently\_powered} for superiority, not evidence of universal inferiority.

Accordingly, we position this work as (i) a retrieval evaluation methodology, (ii) an uncertainty-aware benchmarking framework, and (iii) a compute-aware orchestration framework. We recommend that future IR claims involving small deltas adopt the same discipline before asserting architectural superiority.
