\begin{abstract}
Negative results are important in retrieval science, especially when they arise under claim-ready evaluation conditions. We report an empirical preprint on a mixed-domain benchmark configuration in which Kalman-style uncertainty-weighted fusion did not outperform simple mean fusion. On the canonical test split (1193 paired queries; validation split: 634; minimum per-domain test count: 126), MeanFuser reached nDCG@10 of 0.0943 whereas KalmanorixFuser reached 0.0941 (difference: $-0.000203$). The Holm-adjusted p-value for nDCG@10 was 1.0, and the Kalman/Mean latency ratio was approximately 1.022. A hard routing baseline achieved nDCG@10 of 0.0979 and ranked first among observed baselines by nDCG@10. The pre-declared confirmatory slice had zero eligible queries, so it could not support inferential claims; consequently, the hard claim gate decision was blocked. Our claim is deliberately narrow: in this benchmark and fast-local deterministic embedding configuration, uncertainty-weighted Kalman fusion offered no practical or statistically significant advantage over mean fusion, while hard routing was the strongest observed baseline.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Fusion schemes for retrieval are often motivated by the intuition that uncertainty-aware weighting should improve robustness over naive averaging. Kalman filtering provides a principled linear-Gaussian framework for state estimation under uncertainty \cite{kalman1960}. In retrieval, however, whether this inductive bias translates into measurable quality gains depends on data regime, routing behaviour, embedding quality, and evaluation protocol.

This paper reports a negative result from the Kalmanorix canonical mixed-domain benchmark run. The benchmark status is \texttt{claim\_ready}, indicating adequate sample-size and detectable-effect headroom for stronger interpretation under the project rules. Yet the central superiority claim (Kalman beats mean) did not pass: the observed nDCG@10 delta was near zero and non-significant after multiple-comparison correction.

We emphasise scope. We do \emph{not} claim Kalman-style fusion is universally inferior. We claim only that in this benchmark configuration and fast-local deterministic hash embedding mode, we find no practical or statistically significant advantage over mean fusion.

\section{Related Work}
The methodological ingredients are standard. Kalman filtering originates in linear estimation theory \cite{kalman1960}. Retrieval quality is assessed with graded-relevance ranking metrics such as nDCG \cite{jarvelin2002}. BEIR provides a heterogeneous zero-shot retrieval benchmark family that motivates cross-domain stress-testing \cite{thakur2021beir}. For inferential testing under paired query outcomes we use the Wilcoxon signed-rank framework \cite{wilcoxon1945}, bootstrap confidence intervals \cite{efron1994}, and Holm correction for family-wise error control \cite{holm1979}.

Because embedding choice can dominate downstream fusion effects, we separate this evaluation's fast-local deterministic hash embedding mode from neural embedding contexts (e.g., Sentence-BERT \cite{reimers2019}). The present result should not be conflated with neural-encoder evaluations.

\section{Benchmark and Experimental Protocol}
\subsection{Data and benchmark construction}
The evaluated artefact is a mixed-domain benchmark manifest (version \texttt{mixed\_beir\_v1.2.0}) built from BEIR subsets, including NQ, SciFact, FiQA, ArguAna, FEVER, and DBPedia Entity. The manifest records dataset provenance, split policy, and artefact hashes. The canonical report evaluates the \texttt{test} split with available counts train=5078, validation=634, test=1193.

\subsection{Evidence readiness and gating}
The canonical report marks benchmark status as \texttt{claim\_ready}. Sample adequacy checks indicate: 1193 paired test queries, 634 validation queries, and minimum per-domain test count of 126. These exceed minimum thresholds used for claim-readiness in the project.

A stricter hard claim gate is applied to the statement \emph{``Kalman fusion beats mean fusion''}. This gate additionally requires a supported pre-declared confirmatory slice and supported baseline comparisons; in this run, the final hard gate decision is \texttt{blocked}.

\subsection{Primary comparison and statistics}
The primary quality metric is nDCG@10. We report bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals and paired significance with multiplicity correction (Holm). The confirmatory slice is pre-declared by disagreement, uncertainty spread, router confidence, and multi-domain constraints; however, the slice includes zero queries in this run.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Fusion and routing baselines}
We compare:\
(i) \textbf{MeanFuser}: unweighted averaging across specialist signals;\
(ii) \textbf{KalmanorixFuser}: uncertainty-weighted fusion inspired by Kalman update logic;\
(iii) \textbf{Hard routing baseline}: route to a single top specialist.

Additional baselines appear in the canonical artefacts, but these three are central to the claim question.

\subsection{Evaluation setting}
This preprint summarises the canonical fast-local configuration. In this setup, embeddings and retrieval features are generated in a deterministic local mode intended for rapid, reproducible benchmarking. This is distinct from neural embedding evaluation (for example SBERT-style dense encoders), and conclusions here should be interpreted accordingly.

\section{Analysis: Routing Rather Than Fusion}
The strongest observed baseline is hard routing (nDCG@10 = 0.0979), which ranks first by nDCG@10 in the canonical ranking snapshot. This pattern suggests that, for this benchmark configuration, selecting one specialist may be more effective than blending specialist outputs with either simple mean or uncertainty-weighted Kalman fusion.

A plausible interpretation is that when specialist disagreement does not encode recoverable complementary relevance, fusion may dilute strong single-specialist signals. Hard routing may then benefit from decisiveness, especially when routing confidence and specialist specialisation align with domain structure. This interpretation remains hypothesis-generating rather than confirmed mechanism.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Configuration specificity.} Findings are limited to the canonical mixed-domain benchmark and fast-local deterministic embedding mode.
    \item \textbf{External generalisation.} No claim is made about other domains, model families, or production traffic.
    \item \textbf{Correlated metrics.} Multiple ranking metrics are correlated; Holm correction mitigates but does not eliminate interpretive complexity.
    \item \textbf{Confirmatory slice sparsity.} Zero-query confirmatory slice prevents confirmatory inferential support for the central claim.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
In this claim-ready benchmark configuration, Kalmanorix uncertainty-weighted fusion does not beat mean fusion on the primary metric: MeanFuser nDCG@10 is 0.0943, KalmanorixFuser is 0.0941, and the delta is $-0.000203$ with Holm-adjusted p-value 1.0. Hard routing is the strongest observed baseline (nDCG@10 0.0979, first-ranked). The confirmatory slice is empty (0 queries), so the hard claim gate is blocked. The appropriate conclusion is not that Kalman fusion is generally ineffective, but that under this mixed-domain fast-local configuration it provides no demonstrated practical or statistical advantage over mean fusion.
