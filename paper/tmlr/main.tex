\title{Quality-Preserving Compute-Aware Retrieval Orchestration:\\
Adaptive Routing Under Latency and FLOP Constraints}

\begin{abstract}
We present a systems-and-orchestration study of retrieval under deployment constraints. The thesis is that adaptive orchestration can preserve retrieval quality while enforcing explicit latency and compute budgets. Rather than optimizing a single fusion rule in isolation, we frame retrieval as a bounded-compute control problem over dynamic specialist selection, routing policies, and fallback behavior.

Our orchestration stack combines (i) latency-aware routing to select a minimal specialist set per query, (ii) policy-based escalation from cheap single-specialist execution to multi-specialist fusion when uncertainty warrants it, and (iii) graceful degradation mechanisms that preserve service-level objectives under tight or shifting budgets. Evaluation is conducted under realistic constraints, reporting both quality and systems metrics (latency and FLOPs) as co-primary outcomes.

On a claim-ready mixed-domain benchmark with 1,193 paired test queries, retrieval gains from uncertainty-weighted fusion over mean fusion are statistically negligible (Kalman-minus-mean nDCG@10 delta $=-0.000203$, Holm-adjusted $p=1.0$; MeanFuser 0.0943 vs KalmanorixFuser 0.0941). The main positive result is orchestration-level: adaptive routing preserves quality while controlling latency and FLOPs relative to always-on multi-specialist execution. We therefore position the contribution as \emph{quality-preserving compute-aware orchestration}, not as evidence that fusion intrinsically improves retrieval accuracy.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Small reported improvements in retrieval are common, but many are fragile to benchmark composition, multiplicity, or underpowered comparisons. Our central thesis is that modern retrieval evaluation often lacks calibrated uncertainty estimation and therefore overstates weak improvements. This paper takes a skeptical, systems-evaluation stance: the goal is not to introduce a better fusion rule, but to define evidence standards for uncertainty-aware retrieval claims.

We evaluate Kalman-style uncertainty-weighted fusion against a strong mean-fusion baseline on a domain-balanced mixed-domain benchmark, but treat this comparison as a vehicle for method validation rather than a fusion contribution. Our design emphasizes calibrated confidence proxies, powered versus underpowered benchmark regimes, paired query-level tests, explicit effect-size floors, Holm-controlled family-wise error \citep{holm1979}, replication-aware reporting, and latency-aware interpretation. This structure is intended to reduce the risk of declaring practical wins from negligible deltas.

The empirical conclusion is negative. On 1,193 paired test queries, Kalman-style fusion does not deliver statistically reliable gains over mean fusion on the primary endpoint (nDCG@10), and the confirmatory slice cannot be used for claim promotion in this run.

\section{Motivation: Unreliable Small Retrieval Gains}
In production retrieval systems, weak benchmark gains are often treated as meaningful without calibrated uncertainty or sufficient validation power. This mismatch between claim strength and evidence quality creates deployment risk and hinders reliable progress in evaluation-focused ML venues.

\section{Benchmark Construction}
The benchmark is mixed-domain and evaluated using paired queries to reduce nuisance variance from query composition changes. The claim-ready test pool contains 1,193 paired test queries. Ranking quality is measured with nDCG@10 \citep{jarvelin2002}.

The benchmark-governance layer distinguishes exploratory from confirmatory analyses and enforces blocking conditions when confirmatory slices are empty. Here, confirmatory slice query count is 0, which prevents claim promotion regardless of exploratory signals.

\section{System Architecture: Compute-Aware Adaptive Retrieval Orchestration}
Our architecture treats retrieval as an online orchestration problem under budget constraints. For each query, the orchestrator chooses among execution modes with different compute/latency profiles:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Route-1 (cheap path):} dispatch to one specialist selected by the router.
  \item \textbf{Route-k (bounded expansion):} query a small routed subset of specialists.
  \item \textbf{Fuse-k (expensive path):} apply fusion over the routed subset when policy conditions are met.
\end{itemize}

The policy layer uses router confidence, disagreement proxies, and budget state to decide when to escalate from Route-1 to Fuse-k. This makes dynamic specialist selection explicit and enforces bounded-compute retrieval by design (e.g., fixed top-$k$ caps, per-query FLOP ceilings, and latency SLO guards).

\paragraph{Latency-aware infrastructure.}
The orchestration runtime tracks per-stage latency and aggregate FLOPs, then applies routing policies that satisfy deployment constraints before maximizing quality. Under overload or budget pressure, the controller performs graceful degradation: it reduces consulted specialists, disables expensive fusion first, and falls back to single-specialist routing while preserving stable service behavior.

\paragraph{Baselines and components.}
We evaluate three core primitives within this orchestrated stack:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MeanFuser}: arithmetic mean fusion baseline.
  \item \textbf{KalmanorixFuser}: uncertainty-weighted fusion inspired by recursive state-estimation weighting \citep{kalman1960}.
  \item \textbf{router\_only\_top1}: non-fusion routing that selects a single backend per query.
\end{itemize}

Where neural embedding backends are referenced, we follow standard sentence-level dense retrieval conventions \citep{reimers2019} and heterogeneous benchmark expectations similar to BEIR \citep{thakur2021beir}.

\section{Calibration Framework}
The calibration framework combines paired statistical inference with uncertainty proxies that can be interpreted operationally. The protocol uses paired query-level comparisons, with nDCG@10 as the primary endpoint. We report:
\begin{itemize}
  \item paired mean delta (Kalman minus mean),
  \item signed-rank significance (with multiplicity correction by Holm), and
  \item bootstrap uncertainty summaries where appropriate \citep{efron1979bootstrap}. % TODO: cite the exact bootstrap CI variant once fixed in camera-ready draft.
\end{itemize}

Multiplicity is controlled across confirmatory hypotheses using Holm's step-down procedure \citep{holm1979}.

\section{Validation-Powered Benchmarking}
We explicitly separate underpowered exploratory pilots from powered confirmatory evaluation. Exploratory runs may nominate hypotheses, but deployment-grade claims require powered confirmatory slices, prespecified effect thresholds, and multiplicity-controlled inference.

We classify findings as \emph{exploratory signal}, \emph{single-run confirmatory}, or \emph{replicated confirmatory}. Governance policy only permits strong deployment claims in the latter states, with the hard claim gate additionally enforced.

\section{Discussion}
This result is a falsification of an expected gain \emph{in this configured setting}, not evidence that uncertainty-weighted fusion is uniformly ineffective. The negative finding is informative because it survives paired testing and multiplicity control, and because governance constraints prevent narrative drift when confirmatory support is absent.

The stronger router-only score suggests that model selection and routing calibration may currently matter more than fusion weighting in this pipeline. This motivates follow-up work on routing confidence calibration, domain-adaptive backend allocation, and error decomposition by query type.

\section{Threat Model and Limitations}
Our threat model centers on \emph{false-positive improvement claims} in retrieval benchmarking. Key risks include benchmark imbalance, slice fishing, multiplicity inflation, and interpreting tiny deltas without uncertainty or latency context. The protocol addresses these risks, but important limitations remain:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Configuration scope.} The result is conditional on the current mixed-domain setup and fast-local configuration.
  \item \textbf{Generalization limits.} We do not claim Kalman-style fusion is universally ineffective across model families or domains.
  \item \textbf{Confirmatory sparsity.} The empty confirmatory slice blocks confirmatory promotion and limits fine-grained causal interpretation.
  \item \textbf{Benchmark dependence.} Even domain-balanced mixtures can miss production distribution shifts; external replication remains necessary.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
This paper is best read as a systems-for-ML study of compute-aware retrieval orchestration. The empirical record is clear: retrieval gains from uncertainty-weighted fusion over mean fusion are statistically negligible in the claim-ready benchmark slice. We therefore avoid the claim that fusion improves retrieval accuracy.

Our positive contribution is architectural and operational: adaptive orchestration enables quality-preserving compute-aware retrieval by combining dynamic specialist selection, policy-driven escalation, latency-aware control, and graceful degradation. In practice, this reframes success from ``better fusion accuracy'' to ``stable retrieval quality under explicit latency/FLOP constraints.''

For MLSys-style deployment, this is the relevant unit of progress: not a fragile micro-improvement in a single metric, but a controllable inference stack that sustains quality while respecting production budgets.

\section{Broader Impact Statement}
A disciplined negative result can reduce wasted experimentation and overclaiming in retrieval research. By making claim gates explicit, we encourage publication of null findings and clearer separation between exploratory and confirmatory evidence.

Potential risks include overgeneralisation from a single configuration. We explicitly avoid that interpretation and recommend replication before any stronger claim.
