\begin{abstract}
This paper presents an evaluation methodology for small retrieval improvements where overclaim risk is high. We run a confirmatory paired test of Kalman-style uncertainty fusion versus mean fusion on a mixed-domain benchmark and focus on decision protocol rather than model novelty. Under the tested configuration ($n_{\text{pairs}}=\ConfirmPairs$, \texttt{max\_candidates}=\CandidateCap), Kalman-minus-mean $\Delta$ nDCG@10 is \(\DeltaNDCG\), Holm-adjusted \(p=\HolmP\), and recall@100 delta is \(\DeltaRecall\). Latency is higher (ratio \LatencyRatio) at matched FLOPs (ratio \FlopsRatio). Expanded baseline, ablation, and robustness evidence supports a constrained negative result: no reliable benefit is demonstrated under this protocol. The contribution is a reusable claim-gated template with paired inference, practical-threshold checks, and artifact-backed reproducibility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Modern IR papers often report tiny metric deltas that are sensitive to sampling and benchmark composition. This submission targets that evaluation problem directly: how should one decide whether a small observed improvement is claim-worthy?

Our confirmatory question is narrow: does Kalman-style uncertainty fusion improve nDCG@10 over mean fusion in a domain-balanced mixed-domain setting? We use a predeclared claim gate: positive direction, practical effect size, multiplicity-adjusted significance, and acceptable efficiency overhead must all pass.

\paragraph{Scope of claim.}
We do \textbf{not} claim Kalman fusion is generally ineffective. We claim only that this study did not demonstrate a reliable Kalman-over-mean benefit under the tested protocol.

\section{Protocol and Statistical Framework}
\textbf{Primary endpoint:} paired query-level $\Delta$ nDCG@10 (Kalman minus mean). Secondary endpoints are $\Delta$ recall@100, latency ratio, and FLOPs ratio.

\textbf{Design:} domain-balanced paired evaluation so each query is scored by both methods; confirmatory and exploratory slices are separated.

\textbf{Inference:} paired Wilcoxon testing, Holm correction, and bootstrap interval summaries.

\textbf{Decision rule:} a positive claim requires (i) $\Delta$ nDCG@10 $>0$, (ii) $|\Delta|\ge \PracticalDelta$, (iii) Holm-adjusted $p<0.05$, and (iv) efficiency constraints passing.

The baseline set is intentionally strong rather than strawman. In the canonical matrix, hard routing (top-1) achieves the highest nDCG@10 (0.0983), while mean, Kalman, and fixed-weight fusion are effectively tied at 0.0918-level quality, making the null comparison against mean a meaningful stress test instead of an easy win target.

The overall paired test ($n_{\text{pairs}}=\ConfirmPairs$) is sufficiently powered: $\Delta$ nDCG@10 is near zero (\(\DeltaNDCG\)), Holm-adjusted significance is absent (\(p=\HolmP\)), and recall change is zero. The correct interpretation is therefore \emph{no demonstrated reliable benefit} under the tested gate.

The preregistered confirmatory slice (high disagreement, high uncertainty, multi-domain, low router confidence) contained only $n_{\text{pairs}}=\ConfirmSlicePairs$ query pair, below the minimum of 20 required for inferential claims. Its verdict is \emph{inconclusive\_underpowered}; this is a power limitation at slice level, not a reversal of the overall null.

\section{Ablations and Mechanistic Decomposition}
\input{tables/negative_result_diagnostics}

\paragraph{Uncertainty-quality analysis.}
Uncertainty ablations vary calibration diagnostics but do not produce corresponding retrieval gains: recall@1 and MRR@10 remain effectively unchanged across tested uncertainty methods in \texttt{results/uncertainty\_ablation/summary.json}. This directly addresses whether uncertainty signal quality was meaningful for ranking utility here: it was measurable, but not decision-relevant at the endpoint scale.

\paragraph{Routing-versus-fusion decomposition.}
Evidence from both canonical and routing-eval artifacts indicates the larger variance contribution comes from routing policy, not fusion weighting. Router-only top-1 outruns all fusion variants on nDCG@10 in the canonical matrix, and threshold sweeps materially shift F1/efficiency trade-offs. By contrast, fusion-family deltas (mean, Kalman, fixed-weight) remain tightly clustered.

\paragraph{Specialist diversity interpretation.}
The mixed-domain specialist pool appears to provide value primarily through \emph{selection diversity} (which expert is chosen) rather than \emph{post-selection weighting}. This interpretation is consistent with stronger routing sensitivity and near-flat fusion sensitivity: diversity helps when routing captures complementary specialists, but uncertainty-weighted recombination adds little once candidate experts are fixed.

\section{Conclusion}
This TMLR submission contributes an evaluation protocol for small retrieval deltas centered on rigorous gating, practical significance, and reproducibility. Across strong baselines, uncertainty/covariance ablations, and routing decompositions, Kalman-style fusion does not show a reliable advantage over mean fusion in this mixed-domain confirmatory setting. The preserved negative result is therefore informative: it narrows valid claims, identifies where performance sensitivity actually resides (routing), and provides reusable evidence standards for future retrieval studies.
