\begin{abstract}
This paper presents an evaluation methodology for small retrieval improvements where overclaim risk is high. We run a confirmatory paired test of Kalman-style uncertainty fusion versus mean fusion on a mixed-domain benchmark and focus on decision protocol rather than model novelty. Under the tested configuration ($n_{\text{pairs}}=\ConfirmPairs$, \texttt{max\_candidates}=\CandidateCap), Kalman-minus-mean $\Delta$ nDCG@10 is \(\DeltaNDCG\), Holm-adjusted \(p=\HolmP\), and recall@100 delta is \(\DeltaRecall\). Latency is higher (ratio \LatencyRatio) at matched FLOPs (ratio \FlopsRatio). We therefore report a constrained negative result: no reliable benefit is demonstrated under this protocol. The contribution is a reusable claim-gated template with paired inference, baseline-matrix reporting, and artifact-backed reproducibility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Modern IR papers often report tiny metric deltas that are sensitive to sampling and benchmark composition. This submission targets that evaluation problem directly: how should one decide whether a small observed improvement is claim-worthy?

Our confirmatory question is narrow: does Kalman-style uncertainty fusion improve nDCG@10 over mean fusion in a domain-balanced mixed-domain setting? We use a predeclared claim gate: positive direction, practical effect size, multiplicity-adjusted significance, and acceptable efficiency overhead must all pass.

\paragraph{Scope of claim.}
We do \textbf{not} claim Kalman fusion is generally ineffective. We claim only that this study did not demonstrate a reliable Kalman-over-mean benefit under the tested protocol.

\section{Protocol and Statistical Framework}
\textbf{Primary endpoint:} paired query-level $\Delta$ nDCG@10 (Kalman minus mean). Secondary endpoints are $\Delta$ recall@100, latency ratio, and FLOPs ratio.

\textbf{Design:} domain-balanced paired evaluation so each query is scored by both methods; confirmatory and exploratory slices are separated.

\textbf{Inference:} paired Wilcoxon testing, Holm correction, and bootstrap interval summaries.

\textbf{Decision rule:} a positive claim requires (i) $\Delta$ nDCG@10 $>0$, (ii) $|\Delta|\ge \PracticalDelta$, (iii) Holm-adjusted $p<0.05$, and (iv) efficiency constraints passing.

\section{Why Did Kalman Not Help Here?}
The artifacts support five bounded explanations:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Uncertainty signal weakness.} Query-level uncertainty features did not produce stable ranking gains over a strong mean baseline.
  \item \textbf{Limited downstream effect of calibration.} Calibration analyses showed little translation from uncertainty adjustment to retrieval metric movement.
  \item \textbf{Covariance richness added latency.} Richer covariance handling increased runtime overhead without measurable quality gain.
  \item \textbf{Mean fusion remained competitive.} Baseline-matrix results show near-tied quality against simpler fusion.
  \item \textbf{Candidate cap and benchmark scope.} With \texttt{max\_candidates}=\CandidateCap and benchmark \CanonicalBenchVersion, conclusions are protocol-local and may differ at other budgets or domains.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Reviewer-Facing Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Narrow claim target.} The evidence supports only a within-protocol statement, not a universal statement about Kalman methods.
  \item \textbf{Configuration boundedness.} Results are tied to this implementation, this candidate cap, and this mixed-domain benchmark family.
  \item \textbf{External validity still open.} Different retrievers, uncertainty estimators, or larger candidate pools may change outcomes.
  \item \textbf{Single confirmatory track.} Additional independent confirmatory reruns would strengthen robustness.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
This TMLR submission is about methodology: a claim-gated evaluation protocol for small retrieval deltas. In the tested mixed-domain confirmatory setting, Kalman-style fusion did not show a reliable advantage over mean fusion. The recommended scientific conclusion is constrained and artifact-backed: no reliable benefit was demonstrated under the tested protocol.
